💻 Indie Hacker Cookbook

AI as your co-founder, copywriter, researcher, and customer success team — all at once.

💻

"Ship faster. Learn cheaper. Grow without a team."

You're building something alone, or nearly alone. Every hour matters. AI doesn't replace the craft of building a product people love — but it can handle the research, copy, customer synthesis, and thinking that used to require a full team. These 10 recipes are the ones that pay back the most for a solo founder.

Copywriting

The Landing Page Writer

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Before you write a single word of your landing page

Landing page copy is the single highest-leverage writing task for a new product. One great headline can double your conversion rate; a bad one buries you. This recipe forces you to think like a copywriter — benefit-first, specific, honest — and gives you something to actually work from instead of a blank page.

The Recipe
I'm building [product name] — [one plain-English sentence describing what it does]. My target customer is [specific description — not "everyone," be narrow].

Write landing page copy for the hero section:
1. Headline (under 8 words, benefit-focused — NOT "The easiest way to..." or "Introducing...")
2. Subheadline (1–2 sentences: who it's for + what changes for them)
3. Three feature/benefit pairs (feature name + one sentence on the user benefit, not the technical capability)
4. Primary CTA button text (2–5 words, action-oriented)
5. One social proof line — even a placeholder works (e.g., "Join 340 designers who...")

My current draft (if I have one): [paste or leave blank]

Rules: no buzzwords, no "revolutionary" or "game-changing," no passive voice. Write like a person, not a SaaS marketing team.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Indie hackers write their own copy — there's no marketing team. This recipe gives you a structured first draft in minutes instead of hours, and the specificity constraints ("under 8 words," "no buzzwords") prevent the generic slop that kills conversion rates.

Outreach

Cold Email That Gets Replies

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Customer discovery, early sales, partnership outreach

The cold email is the indie hacker's secret weapon for getting early customers and conversations. Most people write terrible ones — generic, self-focused, too long. This recipe forces the right structure: lead with them, not you.

The Recipe
I need to write a cold email to [describe the person — their role, company type, what they care about].

Context about me: [who you are, what you're building, at what stage]
What I want from this email: [one specific ask — a 20-minute call, feedback, a referral, a pilot]
What's in it for them: [be honest — why should they respond?]
One specific thing I know about them (from their content, company, or work): [the personalization hook]

Write a cold email that:
- Opens with something specific to them — not "I love your work" generically
- Gets to the point by line 3
- Leads with what's in it for them, not what I want
- Makes one clear, low-friction ask
- Sounds like one human writing to another, not a template

Maximum 120 words. Every word earns its place.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Customer development is the job before the product. Every early customer, interview, or partnership starts with an ask. Short, personal emails get replies; long, generic ones get ignored. At the indie stage, five good conversations are worth more than five hundred website visitors.

Engineering

Rubber Duck Debugging

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: Anytime you're stuck on a bug or confusing behavior

Building solo means no one to turn to when you're stuck. Rubber duck debugging — explaining your problem out loud to force clarity — is one of the most effective debugging techniques, and AI is a patient, always-available duck that can actually respond.

The Recipe
I'm stuck on a bug and I want to think through it out loud. Please be my rubber duck — ask clarifying questions as needed, don't just hand me the answer.

Here's what I'm trying to do: [what the code should do]
Here's what's actually happening: [the observed behavior]
Here's the error or symptom: [paste error message or describe the symptom]
Here's the relevant code: [paste the code]
Here's what I've already tried: [list your attempts]

Don't give me the fix immediately. First, ask me the questions a senior engineer would ask to understand the problem better. Then, once we've talked through it, suggest where to look.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Solo developers lose hours to bugs that a 10-minute conversation would have solved. The key instruction here — "don't give me the answer immediately" — forces the dialogue that actually helps you understand the problem rather than just copy-pasting a fix you don't fully get.

Product

Feature Prioritization Matrix

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Whenever your backlog is growing faster than your capacity

Knowing what not to build is the hard part. This recipe gives you a framework for thinking through a list of features — not just impact vs. effort, but against your specific stage and what you're actually uncertain about.

The Recipe
I'm building [product] and I have too many things I could work on. Here's my list:
[paste your feature/improvement list]

My current stage: [pre-launch / launched, X paying customers / growing past initial traction]
Biggest constraint: [time / technical complexity / uncertainty about what users want / money]
My ICP: [specific description]
Current biggest problem in the product: [what do users complain about most, or where do you lose them]

For each item, evaluate:
1. Impact on retention (does it keep users coming back?)
2. Impact on activation (does it help new users get value faster?)
3. Effort (hours / days / weeks — be honest)
4. Confidence level (how certain are we this is what users actually want?)

Give me a prioritized list with brief reasoning. Be direct: if something should be cut entirely or deferred indefinitely, say so. I need a clear top-3 to work on next, not 15 things "worth considering."

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The indie hacker's curse is building features nobody asked for. This recipe forces you to map your own constraints before asking for help — and the explicit "cut it entirely" instruction prevents the polite non-answer that leaves you no clearer than before.

Research

User Interview Distiller

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: After every batch of 3+ customer conversations

Customer interviews are full of signal, but messy. Synthesizing 5 conversations into clear product insights takes hours when you do it manually. This recipe turns raw notes into a structured picture of what matters — and catches patterns you'd miss by reading linearly.

The Recipe
I just finished [X] customer conversations. Here are my notes:

[PASTE YOUR INTERVIEW NOTES — even rough ones work]

Please analyze and give me:
1. The 3–5 most common pain points (ranked by frequency and emotional intensity)
2. The exact phrases and words they used most — verbatim, in quotes (steal these for copy)
3. The job they're actually hiring this product to do (what outcome are they really after?)
4. The biggest surprise — something I might not have expected
5. The top 3 hypotheses I should test next based on what I heard

Don't over-interpret thin data. If two people said something and three said something different, tell me that instead of picking a winner. I want signal, not spin.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Most indie hackers do customer interviews, few systematically synthesize them. The "verbatim phrases" instruction is particularly valuable — those exact words become your headline copy, your sales pitch, your Twitter bio. Your customers tell you how to sell to them; this recipe helps you hear it.

Strategy

Competitor Teardown

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Sous Chef ⏱️ When to use: Before defining your positioning, or when you're losing to a competitor

You don't need to beat every competitor — you need to find the customers they're not serving well and own that territory. This recipe maps the competitive landscape so you can see the gaps clearly rather than just comparing feature lists.

The Recipe
I want to understand my competitive landscape. Here's my context:

My product: [what it does, for whom]
My ICP: [specific description]
Competitor to analyze: [name, URL, or description if they don't have a public presence]

Analyze this competitor across:
1. Positioning — who they say they're for, and what promise they make
2. Pricing and business model
3. What they're clearly optimized for (what they're really good at)
4. What they seem to be ignoring or doing poorly
5. The type of customer they're NOT built for — who falls through their cracks

Then tell me: what's the most defensible positioning gap for a product like mine, given what you know about my ICP?

Be analytical. I don't need "here's your opportunity to win!" — I need an honest competitive map.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Indie hackers often position vaguely ("it's simpler and more affordable") because they haven't done the hard work of mapping where competitors genuinely fall short. The "type of customer they're NOT built for" question is the one that actually unlocks useful positioning.

Launch

Launch Announcement Writer

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Before a Product Hunt, HN, or community launch

Launch day is high-stakes — bad copy buries momentum before it starts. The indie launch post has a specific formula that works: start with the problem, not the product. Lead with the story, not the feature list. This recipe builds that structure for any platform.

The Recipe
I'm launching [product name] on [platform: Product Hunt / Hacker News / Twitter / a newsletter].

What it does: [1–2 sentences, plain English, no jargon]
Who it's for: [specific — not "everyone who wants to be more productive"]
The problem it solves: [describe the pain vividly — what does it feel like before this exists?]
What makes it different: [honest, specific — not "it's simple and powerful"]
My story: [why you built this — briefly]
Current state: [free beta / $X/month / what the free tier includes if any]

Write a [Product Hunt description / Show HN post / launch tweet thread] that:
- Opens with the problem or a moment, not the product name
- Sounds like a builder talking to builders, not a marketer
- Is honest about what it is and what it isn't yet
- Ends with a clear, specific, low-friction CTA

Avoid: "excited to announce," "game-changing," superlatives, passive voice, anything that sounds like a press release.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Indie hacker communities (Product Hunt, HN, Twitter) have finely tuned detectors for corporate-sounding copy. The best indie launches sound like one person talking honestly about something they built. This recipe gets you there instead of the over-polished version that reads as fake.

Research

The Mom Test Question Generator

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Sous Chef ⏱️ When to use: Before your first customer development calls

Bad customer interview questions get you polite lies. "Would you use this?" — yes. "Would you pay for this?" — probably. The Mom Test framework (ask about their life, not your idea) gets you the truth. This recipe generates the right questions and flags the wrong ones before you waste calls on misleading signal.

The Recipe
I'm building [product/idea] for [target customer]. I'm setting up customer discovery interviews.

Using the principles from "The Mom Test" — ask about their life, not your idea; seek facts and past behavior, not opinions about hypothetical futures:

Generate 15 interview questions that help me understand:
1. Whether this problem actually exists and how painful it is
2. How they currently solve it (cost in time, money, or frustration)
3. What a solution would need to do to be worth switching for
4. Whether I'm talking to the right person (buyer vs. user vs. influencer)

For each question, flag any that are leading, too hypothetical, or likely to get polite non-answers — and suggest a better version.

Also: what are 3 topics I should NOT ask about directly, because they'll give me misleading signal? And why?

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Most indie hackers do customer interviews wrong and come away feeling validated when they should feel uncertain. The Mom Test is the antidote. Generating and reviewing your questions before calls is a discipline most skip — and it's where the most expensive mistakes happen.

Productivity

Weekly Retrospective

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: End of every work week — 15 minutes is enough

Building alone is hard to navigate without the accountability and feedback loops that come from a team. A weekly retro with AI as your thinking partner forces you to look at what's actually happening — not just feel busy — and decide deliberately what to focus on next.

The Recipe
End of week. Help me run a solo founder retrospective.

What I shipped or completed this week: [list]
What I didn't finish (and the honest reason): [list]
Key metrics: [signups / revenue / conversations / whatever matters — even if the numbers are bad]
Something that surprised me: [optional]
Something I'm uncertain about or avoiding: [optional]

Based on this, help me:
1. Name the single most important thing to focus on next week (not a list of 5 — one thing)
2. Spot any patterns — am I consistently blocked by the same thing?
3. Draft 3 specific, achievable goals for next week
4. Call out anything I might be rationalizing or procrastinating on

Be direct. I don't need encouragement — I need clarity about what's actually going on and what to do next.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Without a co-founder or team, it's easy to stay "busy" while drifting away from what matters. The "something you're avoiding" prompt is the most valuable line — that's often where the most important work hides.

Engineering

Tech Stack Advisor

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Before any significant infrastructure or tooling decision

Tech decisions carry disproportionate long-term consequences for indie projects. The wrong framework choice can cost you months. This recipe forces a decision that's optimized for your context — not for what's trendy or what works at scale you'll never reach.

The Recipe
I need to make a tech decision for my indie project. Here's my context:

What I'm building: [description]
The specific decision: [hosting / database / auth / framework / payment integration / etc.]
My technical background: [languages and frameworks I know well vs. passably]
Realistic scale in 6 months: [honest estimate — don't plan for a million users if you have 50]
Budget: [monthly spend I can accept]
Time I have for this decision: [can I afford a complex setup, or do I need to ship fast?]
Current stack (if any): [list what you're already using]

Give me 2–3 sensible options for my situation with honest tradeoffs. Don't list everything that exists — curate for a solo founder. If one option is clearly better for my constraints, say so directly instead of "it depends." I'm making a real decision, not writing a blog post.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The temptation to over-engineer is strong, especially for developers. The "realistic scale in 6 months" and "time I have" fields force an honest conversation about what the decision actually needs to optimize for — which, at the indie stage, is almost always "ship it now and don't paint yourself into a corner."
