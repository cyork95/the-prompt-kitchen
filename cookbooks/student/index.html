✏️ Student Cookbook

AI as your personal tutor, writing coach, and study partner — without it doing the work for you.

✏️

"Study smarter, write better, understand anything."

These recipes treat AI as a tutor that pushes back, a writing coach that points to the specific sentence that's weak, and a study partner that won't just tell you the answer. The goal is to make you better — not to do it for you.

👤 High school & university students 🎯 Studying, writing, research 📋 8 recipes
A note on academic integrity: Every recipe here is designed to improve your thinking, not replace it. The prompts ask AI to critique your work, quiz you, or map a topic — not to write essays or generate answers you submit as your own. Know your institution's AI policy; these tools are most valuable when you're in the driver's seat.

Understanding

Teach Me Like I'm [Level]

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: First contact with any new concept you don't understand

Google gives you information. AI gives you an explanation calibrated to exactly where you are. The key is being honest about your level — "explain it like I'm a first-year who hasn't taken linear algebra" gets a dramatically better answer than "explain eigenvalues."

The Recipe
Please explain [concept] to me. Here's where I'm starting from:
- My level: [year in school, subject background — e.g., "second-year biology student, no chemistry background"]
- What I already know about this: [even if it's very little]
- What's specifically confusing me: [the bit that isn't clicking]
- Why I need to understand this: [for an exam / for a paper / just curious — this shapes how deep to go]

Please:
1. Start with the core idea in plain language — one paragraph, no jargon
2. Give me a concrete analogy or example from everyday life
3. Build up to the technical version once the intuition is there
4. Check my understanding: give me one question I should be able to answer if I've got it

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Lecturers explain things at their level, not yours. Textbooks explain things at a standardized level. AI explains things at your level — if you tell it where you are. The final "check my understanding" question turns this from passive reading into active learning.

Studying

Study Guide Builder

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: After a lecture or before starting to review a chapter

Raw notes and textbook chapters are information, not study material. This recipe turns them into something you can actually use — structured for active recall rather than passive re-reading, which is the most evidence-backed way to actually retain things.

The Recipe
I need to turn the following material into an active study guide.

[PASTE YOUR LECTURE NOTES OR CHAPTER TEXT]

Please create a study guide with:
1. Key concepts — name + one plain-English definition (no jargon unless the jargon is what I need to learn)
2. The most important relationships — how the key concepts connect to each other
3. The tricky parts — common misconceptions or confusing points to watch for
4. 5 practice questions at varied difficulty (easy / medium / hard — with answers at the bottom)
5. A "last-minute review" priority list — if I have 20 minutes before the exam, what are the 5 most important things?

Format it for active recall, not re-reading. Make me work to retrieve the answers.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Most students re-read notes and call it studying. Re-reading gives the feeling of familiarity without the actual recall practice that builds memory. The practice questions in this guide force active retrieval — which means the studying actually sticks.

Writing

The Essay Critique

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: After a first draft, before your final submission

Getting real feedback on academic writing before you submit is rare — office hours are limited and peers are often too polite. This recipe gets you specific, actionable critique on your actual argument structure, not just grammar and formatting.

The Recipe
Please give me detailed feedback on this essay draft.

Context: I'm a [year, e.g., "second-year history student"]. The assignment: [describe briefly — word limit, specific question, format].

[PASTE YOUR ESSAY DRAFT]

Critique it on:
1. Argument structure — is my thesis clear? Does each paragraph advance it, or does anything go off-track?
2. Evidence quality — are my claims supported? Are sources used correctly, or am I asserting things I haven't proven?
3. Logical consistency — are there any gaps, contradictions, or leaps in the reasoning?
4. Clarity — which sentences or paragraphs are vague, dense, or confusing?
5. "Telling vs. analyzing" — am I describing what happened, or am I arguing why it matters?

Be specific: point to actual sentences or paragraphs. Don't just say "the introduction is weak" — tell me which sentence is the problem and why.

Don't rewrite it for me. Help me understand what to fix and why.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The "don't rewrite it for me" instruction is the most important line. This recipe is a feedback tool, not a ghostwriting tool. The distinction between "telling" and "analyzing" is the most common mistake in academic writing — and the one most students don't know they're making.

Studying

Exam Simulator

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: In the days before an exam — especially for topics you're less confident on

Active recall — testing yourself rather than re-reading — is one of the most evidence-backed study techniques. This recipe turns AI into a patient, adaptive quiz partner that adjusts difficulty based on your answers and focuses where you need it most.

The Recipe
I have an exam on [subject/topic] in [timeframe]. Please be my exam simulator.

Exam format: [multiple choice / short answer / essay questions / problem sets — or a mix]
Topics covered: [list]
My confidence by topic:
- [Topic A]: [high / medium / low / I have no idea]
- [Topic B]: [high / medium / low]
- [etc.]

Rules for our session:
- Start with one question at [beginner / intermediate / challenging] level
- After I answer: tell me if I'm right or wrong
  → If wrong: explain exactly where my thinking went off, then ask a follow-up to check I've got the correction
  → If right: acknowledge briefly, then push deeper with a harder question
- Focus most of your questions on the topics where my confidence is lowest
- Do NOT give me the answer before I've attempted it — if I'm stuck, give me a hint first

Let's start.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The "give me a hint before the answer" instruction is critical — it keeps you in active problem-solving mode rather than passive answer-receiving mode. An AI that explains everything immediately trains you to be helpless; one that pushes back trains you to think. Ask for the latter.

Writing

Counter-Argument Finder

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Sous Chef ⏱️ When to use: After your first draft, before you finalize your argument

Strong academic writing doesn't just make a case — it anticipates and addresses objections. Most students skip this step. This recipe finds the holes in your argument so you can address them before your professor marks them in red.

The Recipe
Here is my thesis and main argument for an upcoming paper:

[PASTE YOUR THESIS AND SUPPORTING ARGUMENT]

This is for [subject and level — e.g., "a second-year political theory essay"].

Please identify:
1. The 3 strongest counter-arguments a critical reader would raise
2. For each: what evidence or perspective supports it (be specific, not vague)
3. For each: should I refute it, concede it partially, or reframe my thesis to account for it?
4. Any unstated assumptions in my argument that a skeptic would attack
5. One question my argument doesn't answer that a reader would reasonably expect it to

Be the toughest critic, not a supportive one. My grade depends on anticipating these objections before my professor does — not on feeling good about my draft.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The difference between a B paper and an A paper is almost always whether the argument engages seriously with objections. Most students only make their case; the best writers show they've understood why someone smart might disagree, and why they're still right (or why they've updated their view).

Research

Research Rabbit Hole Guide

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: When you're starting research on a topic you don't know well

Starting research without a map is why students end up overwhelmed by Wikipedia for two hours and then can't figure out what's relevant. This recipe gives you a researcher's mental model of a topic before you go deep — so you know what you're looking for.

The Recipe
I'm starting research on [topic] for [context — a seminar paper / a thesis chapter / a presentation].

What I already know: [brief summary — even "very little" is a valid answer]

Please give me a researcher's map of this topic:
1. The 4–5 most important sub-topics or debates within this field
2. The key thinkers or foundational works I need to engage with (and why each one matters)
3. The central unsettled question — what do experts still genuinely disagree about?
4. A suggested reading order — what do I read first to build the right foundation?
5. Two questions I should be asking that I probably haven't thought of yet

Don't give me a Wikipedia overview. Give me the kind of map a PhD student would draw from memory. Point me toward what's actually contested and important, not just what's introductory.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Starting research well is the most underrated skill in academia. The "unsettled question" prompt is particularly valuable — it tells you where the real intellectual action is in a field, which is where strong papers go rather than just summarizing settled knowledge.

Understanding

The Plain English Translator

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: Whenever you're reading something dense and getting lost

Academic writing is often unnecessarily obscure. Some of that obscurity is meaningful precision — some of it is just academic writing style. This recipe cuts through both and gives you what the text is actually saying, so you can engage with the idea rather than struggling with the prose.

The Recipe
Here is an academic text I'm struggling to understand:

[PASTE THE TEXT]

Please:
1. Explain in plain English what the author is actually arguing (not summarizing — explaining)
2. Identify the key claim and the main evidence or reasoning they use to support it
3. Explain any technical terms or concepts I need to know to follow the argument
4. Tell me: why does this argument matter? What does it challenge or change?
5. One-sentence version: what is this about?

Write this as if you're explaining it to a smart friend who hasn't read it. Don't reproduce the text — help me understand it.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The "why does this matter?" question is the one that gets skipped when you're just trying to get through the reading. But it's the question you'll need to answer in seminars and essays. Understanding what a text is doing — not just what it says — is the difference between engaging with it and summarizing it.

Planning

The Deadline Planner

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: The moment an assignment is set — not the week before it's due

Most students plan backwards from the deadline and underestimate every phase. This recipe builds an honest, backwards-planned schedule with real buffer — plus a minimum viable version for when everything goes sideways, because at some point it will.

The Recipe
I have the following assignment due in [X weeks / on date]:

Assignment: [what it is — essay / problem set / project / presentation]
Requirements: [word count / sections / specific deliverables]
Other commitments during this period: [other deadlines, exams, work, life]

Create a backwards-planned schedule from the deadline to today:
1. Key milestones with realistic time estimates (research complete, outline done, first draft, feedback round, final draft, proofread)
2. Daily or weekly targets that actually fit my schedule
3. Built-in buffer — assume at least one day will be lost to something unexpected
4. A minimum viable version: if everything goes wrong and I have half the time, what do I submit?

Be honest about timelines. I'd rather know the uncomfortable truth on day one than be surprised on the day before the deadline.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The "minimum viable version" isn't giving up — it's a risk management tool. Knowing what your floor looks like means you can make rational tradeoffs under pressure rather than panicking. The students who do best under deadline pressure aren't the ones who never fall behind; they're the ones who planned for falling behind.
