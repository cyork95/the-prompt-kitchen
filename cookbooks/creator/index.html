🎬 Creator Cookbook

More output. Consistent voice. Less time on the parts that don't require you.

🎬

"Content that sounds like you, scales like a team."

The creator's core problem: there's always more content to make than time to make it. These recipes handle the structural, repeatable parts — outlines, hooks, repurposing, calendars — so your actual time goes to the parts that require your specific perspective and voice.

Distribution

Content Repurposing Machine

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: After every long-form piece you publish

The most efficient content strategy is making one thing and distributing it widely. A 15-minute YouTube video contains enough material for a week of content across platforms — if you know how to pull it apart. This recipe does the extraction for you.

The Recipe
Here is a piece of long-form content I just published:

[PASTE: video transcript / blog post / podcast transcript / newsletter]

My audience across platforms:
- [Platform 1 — e.g., YouTube]: [audience description and what performs well]
- [Platform 2 — e.g., Twitter/X]: [audience description]
- [Platform 3 — e.g., LinkedIn]: [audience description]

Please extract and reformat this into the following pieces:
1. Twitter/X thread (8–10 tweets, hook + value + CTA, each tweet punchy and standalone)
2. LinkedIn post (one insight, thoughtful tone, no hashtag spam, 150–250 words)
3. Email newsletter blurb (100–150 words — what did I learn or make this week, and why should they care?)
4. Instagram/short-form caption (under 80 words, conversational, platform-appropriate)
5. One short-form video script (60–90 seconds — one sharp insight from the original)

For each: preserve my voice. Don't genericize. Keep the specific examples and language from the original wherever possible.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Most creators publish once and move on. The best ones treat every piece as raw material for five more. The "preserve my voice" instruction is critical — without it, AI turns your specific, hard-won perspective into generic content that could have been written by anyone.

Copywriting

The Hook Writer

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Before starting any piece — the hook deserves more than 2 minutes

The hook is 80% of whether anyone reads, watches, or clicks. Most creators spend 95% of their energy on the content and 5% on the hook. This recipe inverts that ratio for the thing that most determines reach — and gives you 10 options to choose from rather than writing one and hoping.

The Recipe
I'm creating content on [topic] for [platform: YouTube / newsletter / Twitter / LinkedIn / podcast].

My audience: [specific description — who they are and what they care about]
The angle/premise: [what you're saying — your take, not just the topic]

Generate 10 different opening hooks for this content:
- 3 question hooks (provocative, non-rhetorical — hooks that create genuine curiosity, not "ever wondered why...?")
- 3 bold statement hooks (counterintuitive or surprising claim — something that makes someone stop scrolling)
- 2 story/scene hooks (open in the middle of something happening, present tense)
- 2 "contrast" hooks (before/after, what everyone thinks vs. what's actually true)

For each, tell me: what specific emotion or curiosity mechanism is it triggering?

Don't give me generic options. Every hook should be specific to this topic and this audience — nothing that could work for any piece about anything.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Having 10 options means you're choosing the best one, not just going with what came to mind first. The "what emotion is it triggering?" analysis trains you to understand why hooks work — so eventually you won't need the recipe, you'll just think this way naturally.

Video

YouTube Script Outliner

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Before every video — starting from a topic, not a blank script

Blank page paralysis kills more video ideas than lack of topics. A strong outline takes 15 minutes and makes scripting or talking-head filming 10x faster. This recipe builds the structure so you can focus your energy on the delivery.

The Recipe
I'm making a YouTube video on: [topic]

My channel is about: [niche in one sentence]
My audience knows: [assumed knowledge level — don't explain things they already know]
My angle / thesis (what I want them to believe or understand by the end): [your specific take]
Target video length: [X minutes]

Create a full video outline with:
1. Hook (first 15 seconds — what one moment, statement, or question will make them stay?)
2. Setup / promise (what will they get from watching — make it specific and compelling)
3. Main sections (3–5, each with: section title, 3–4 talking points, any stats/examples to include)
4. Transitions (what's the narrative thread that connects one section to the next?)
5. CTA / close (what's the one thing you want them to do, think, or feel? Keep it singular)

For sections with visual content: suggest a B-roll idea, on-screen text, or graphic that would strengthen it. Flag any section that risks losing viewer attention — suggest a pattern interrupt.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The "pattern interrupt" flag is the most underused element here. YouTube retention graphs have predictable drop-off points — usually around the minute mark and at each section transition. Knowing where to re-hook the viewer before they leave is a structural skill most creators develop by accident over years. This shortcut gets you there faster.

Voice

Voice Calibration Test

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Sous Chef ⏱️ When to use: When scaling output or onboarding help — to protect your voice from dilution

As creators scale, their voice gets diluted — especially when using AI to draft at higher volume. This recipe teaches AI your specific voice before it writes anything, then flags where it had to make judgment calls so you can correct the calibration over time.

The Recipe
I'm going to give you 3 examples of my writing. Then I'll ask you to write something new in my voice.

Example 1: [paste a piece you're proud of — written by you, not AI]
Example 2: [paste another — ideally from a different format or topic]
Example 3: [paste another]

Step 1 — Before writing anything new, describe my voice back to me:
- How would you describe the tone? (formal/casual/irreverent/warm/direct — be specific)
- What sentence structures do I tend to use? (short punchy ones? Long building ones? A mix?)
- What do I do that most writers in my niche don't?
- What should you avoid to NOT sound like me?

Step 2 — Write [a newsletter intro / tweet thread / blog opening / caption] about [topic] in that voice.

Step 3 — After writing, tell me: what were the 2–3 moments where you had to make a judgment call about my voice, and what did you choose? This helps me correct the calibration.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

Step 3 is the part most people skip, and it's the most valuable. When AI tells you where it guessed about your voice, you learn something about your own style that you probably never articulated explicitly. That meta-awareness helps you write more consistently and brief AI collaborators more effectively.

Research

Comment Section Goldmine

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Once a month — mining your best comments for content intelligence

Your audience already told you what to make next. It's in your comments, DMs, and replies — you just haven't synthesized it. This recipe turns a pile of unread comments into a content strategy, complete with the exact language your audience uses (which is also your best copy).

The Recipe
Here are comments/replies/DMs from my recent content:

[PASTE 15–40 COMMENTS — copy/paste from YouTube, social, email replies, etc.]

Please analyze these and give me:
1. The top 3 questions my audience keeps asking (explicit ones and the implicit ones underneath)
2. What they're frustrated or confused about — read between the lines, not just what they directly said
3. The exact phrases and words they use to describe the problem (these become my copy)
4. What content they're hungry for that I haven't made yet — based on what they're asking for, not what I planned
5. One surprising insight the data suggests about my audience that I might not have consciously noticed

Based on all of this: what's the single highest-priority piece of content I should make next, and why? Give me a specific angle, not just a topic.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The comment section is the most underused research tool in a creator's toolkit. The "exact phrases they use" prompt is worth the whole recipe — your audience's own words are your best headline copy, because they already know it describes their problem. You can't invent that in a brainstorm.

Outreach

The Collaboration Pitch

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Home Cook ⏱️ When to use: Before reaching out to another creator or sponsor

Most creator pitches are terrible — generic, self-focused, and leading with follower count. The ones that get replies lead with genuine value for the other person and a specific, interesting idea. This recipe writes the version that respects the recipient's time.

The Recipe
I want to pitch a collaboration / sponsorship to [person or brand name].

About me: [your platform, niche, audience size and engagement, what you're actually known for]
About them: [what they do, their audience, why their work specifically matters to you — not generic praise]
The collaboration idea: [specific — not "some kind of collab," but what exactly you have in mind and why it fits both audiences]
What's in it for them: [be honest and specific — what value do they actually get?]

Write a pitch email / DM that:
- Opens with something specific and genuine about their work (not "I love what you do")
- Gets to the point within the first 2 sentences
- Leads with what's in it for them — not what I want
- Makes one clear, easy-to-say-yes-to ask
- Sounds like one creator reaching out to another, not a brand doing influencer outreach

Maximum 120 words. Shorter is better. If it doesn't fit in 120 words, the idea isn't clear enough yet.

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The 120-word limit is a forcing function for clarity. Most bad pitches are long because the sender hasn't figured out what they actually want or what they're actually offering. If you can't make your case in 120 words, the pitch isn't ready — not the word count.

Newsletter

Newsletter Template Builder

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Sous Chef ⏱️ When to use: When starting a newsletter or when your format has drifted and you need to reset

Consistent newsletter structure increases opens and reduces writer's block simultaneously — readers know what to expect and you know what to fill in. The goal isn't a rigid template, it's a repeatable rhythm that gives structure without feeling formulaic.

The Recipe
I write a [frequency: weekly / biweekly / monthly] newsletter called [name] for [audience description].

My newsletter's core promise: [what readers get from every issue — be specific]
My writing style: [describe, or paste 1–2 paragraphs from a previous issue]
What's worked in past issues: [what readers have responded to best]
What I want to avoid: [what feels forced or generic in your current format]

Design a reusable newsletter template with these components:
1. Opening (how do you hook them in the first 3 sentences every issue?)
2. Main value section (what structure makes your core content easy to navigate?)
3. A recurring insight or pattern (something readers recognize issue to issue — a "section" they look forward to)
4. A find / recommendation (something you read, tried, or discovered this week)
5. A close (how do you end with personality rather than "that's all for this week"?)

For each section: what's its specific job? What question does it answer for the reader? And what makes it fail (what would make it feel like filler)?

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The "what makes it fail?" question is the one that separates a useful template from a useless one. Most newsletter templates list what to include; this one also tells you what turns each section into dead weight. That's the difference between a template you actually use and one you abandon by issue three.

Planning

Posting Calendar Generator

▾
🎓 Difficulty: Beginner ⏱️ When to use: At the start of each month, before the blank calendar anxiety sets in

Inconsistency is the biggest killer of creator growth — not quality, not topics, not the algorithm. A 4-week calendar you can actually execute is worth more than a 12-week plan you'll abandon. This recipe builds the version you'll actually follow.

The Recipe
I create content about [niche] for [platform(s)].

My posting frequency: [X times per week/month]
My audience: [description of who they are and what they care about]
My content pillars (main recurring themes): [list 3–5]
What's performed well recently: [topics or formats that got engagement]
What I want to explore or haven't tried: [optional]

Generate a 4-week content calendar:
- A specific topic / angle for each post (not just "a post about X" — a real idea with an angle)
- Suggested format (long-form / short / personal story / educational / series episode / etc.)
- Which content pillar it falls under
- A one-line note on why this week makes sense for this topic (seasonal, evergreen, timely)

Rules:
- Spread content across all pillars — don't cluster similar topics
- Include at least 1 personal or story-based post per week
- Flag 2–3 topics that could become a multi-part series
- Be realistic — assume I will sometimes be 20% behind schedule

🔑 Why it's in this cookbook

The "at least 1 personal post per week" rule is in here because it's the instruction creators forget when they get busy. Algorithms reward consistency; audiences reward humanity. The posts that built your following weren't the most optimized ones — they were the ones that felt most like you. Don't optimize that out.
