🥫 The Pantry

Reusable building blocks for better prompts. Mix these modular ingredients into any recipe — or combine them from scratch.

Every great kitchen has a well-stocked pantry — olive oil, garlic, salt — things you reach for in almost every recipe. These prompt blocks work the same way. They're not full recipes; they're reusable fragments that make any prompt more precise, more useful, and more you. Paste one before your main question, append one to shape the output, or snap two together for something tailored.

➕

Prepend it

Paste a Role or Context block before your actual question. It sets the stage before you ask anything.

📎

Append it

Add an Output Format or Guardrail block after your question. It shapes what comes back without rewriting your ask.

🔗

Combine two

Stack a Role + a Context block + an Output Format for a fully composed prompt — no recipe needed.

Expert Role Starters

2 ingredients Give the AI a persona before you ask anything

The Trusted Advisor

▾
👤 Expert Role

Use when you want a direct, opinionated expert perspective — not a neutral overview. Good for strategy, decisions, or anything where you want real talk, not hedges.

 
Act as a trusted senior advisor with deep expertise in [field]. You have a direct, practical style — you give honest assessments rather than safe, hedged answers. When you don't know something, you say so. You're helping me think through [situation], and I want your real perspective, not a list of generic options.

🔗 Try combining with

👤 Trusted Advisor + 📋 Situation Brief + 🚧 The Fence

Paste the Trusted Advisor first, then the Situation Brief to give full context, then your question, then The Fence to block generic advice. Result: a deeply contextual, no-fluff consultation.

The Skeptic in the Room

▾
👤 Expert Role

Use before committing to a plan or pitching an idea. Activates rigorous stress-testing. The AI finds the weak points so your boss, client, or audience doesn't have to.

 
For this conversation, play the role of the most rigorous, skeptical expert in [field]. Your job is not to be negative — it's to find the weakest points in any plan or argument I present. You ask the hard questions that someone being polite would skip. Push back on assumptions. Point out what I might be overlooking. Be specific about where the logic breaks down.

🔗 Try combining with

👤 Skeptic in the Room + 🧠 First Principles Check

First use First Principles Check to strip away assumptions in your plan. Then apply the Skeptic to attack what remains. This two-pass approach catches far more holes than either block alone.

Context & Background Templates

2 ingredients Tell the AI your situation before you ask

The Situation Brief

▾
📋 Context

Paste this at the top of any conversation where the default answer would be too generic. The more you fill in, the less you'll need to course-correct later.

 
Here's my context before I ask my question:
- Who I am: [your role or relevant background]
- What I'm trying to achieve: [goal or desired outcome]
- What I've already tried or know: [prior attempts, existing knowledge]
- Constraints I'm working within: [time, budget, skill level, access]
- What "success" looks like: [specific, concrete outcome]

With that context in mind — [your actual question here].

🔗 Try combining with

📋 Situation Brief + 👤 Trusted Advisor

Open with the Trusted Advisor role, follow with the Situation Brief, then ask your question. The AI knows who it's supposed to be and exactly what you're dealing with — almost no follow-up needed.

The Audience Brief

▾
📋 Context

Use whenever you're writing something for others — emails, presentations, documentation, social posts. Prevents the AI from writing for the average person when your audience is anything but.

 
Before you write anything, keep this audience in mind:
- Who they are: [role, background, age range if relevant]
- What they already know: [assumed knowledge level]
- What they don't know or often misunderstand: [common gaps or misconceptions]
- Their biggest concern or objection: [what makes them skeptical or hesitant]
- The format they respond best to: [casual email / formal report / short bullets / narrative]

Write for them specifically — not for a generic reader.

🔗 Try combining with

📋 Audience Brief + 📐 The Layered Answer

The Audience Brief tells the AI who it's writing for. The Layered Answer gives them a skimmable summary up top and full depth below — perfect for mixed audiences where some people scan and some read everything.

🧠

Chain-of-Thought Starters

2 ingredients Make the AI show its reasoning before it concludes

Think Out Loud

▾
🧠 Chain-of-Thought

Use for complex decisions, analysis, or any time you want to understand the reasoning — not just the output. Also catches the AI making bad assumptions before they become bad answers.

 
Before you give me a final answer, reason through this step by step. Show your working. Consider:
- What are the key factors or variables here?
- What assumptions are you making, and are they justified?
- What are the main alternatives or approaches?
- What are you choosing to prioritize, and why?
- What could go wrong with the approach you're recommending?

Then give me your conclusion and recommendation at the end, clearly labelled.

🔗 Try combining with

🧠 Think Out Loud + 📐 Executive Brief

Let the AI reason fully with Think Out Loud, then ask it to compress the conclusion into an Executive Brief. You get the full reasoning trail for your own understanding, plus a crisp summary you can share with anyone.

First Principles Check

▾
🧠 Chain-of-Thought

Use when you're stuck in conventional thinking, want a fresh angle on a familiar problem, or suspect that "the way it's always done" might not actually be the best way.

 
Before you answer, reason from first principles rather than convention. Don't tell me what people usually do. Instead, ask:
- What is the actual goal here — stripped of assumptions?
- Which constraints are truly fixed versus just assumed to be fixed?
- If we were designing this from scratch, with no existing conventions to follow, what would we do?
- What does the most direct path to the goal look like?

Reason through this, then give me your answer.

🔗 Try combining with

🧠 First Principles Check + 👤 Skeptic in the Room

First, use First Principles to rebuild your approach from scratch without legacy assumptions. Then run the Skeptic to attack what you've come up with. This two-pass combo catches both conventional ruts and logical holes.

Output Format Templates

2 ingredients Control exactly what shape the answer takes

The Executive Brief

▾
📐 Output Format

Use when you need to share output with stakeholders, drop a summary into Slack, or brief someone in under 60 seconds. Forces the AI to prioritize ruthlessly.

 
Format your answer as an Executive Brief with exactly these three sections:

1. Bottom Line Up Front (1–2 sentences — the single most important thing)
2. Key Points (3 bullet points — supporting evidence or key considerations)
3. Recommended Action (1–2 sentences — what should be done and why)

Maximum 200 words total. Be direct. Cut all filler.

🔗 Try combining with

📐 Executive Brief + 🧠 Think Out Loud

Ask the AI to Think Out Loud first so you see the full reasoning, then append this block: "Now compress your conclusion into an Executive Brief." You get the depth and the summary in one response.

The Layered Answer

▾
📐 Output Format

Use when your audience includes both skimmers and deep-readers. The two-layer format means nobody gets either too little or too much. Great for documentation, reports, and team comms.

 
Structure your response in two clearly labelled layers:

Layer 1 — The Short Answer:
2–3 sentences. For someone in a hurry who just needs the key takeaway.

Layer 2 — The Full Picture:
Full detail, context, nuance, and caveats for someone who wants to go deep.

Label each layer clearly. Don't merge them.

🔗 Try combining with

📐 Layered Answer + 📋 Audience Brief

The Audience Brief tells the AI who it's writing for. Then Layered Answer ensures the busy executive gets the top layer while the detail-hungry analyst gets the second. One response, two audiences served.

Tone & Style References

2 ingredients Dial in exactly how the output should sound

Plain English Please

▾
🎨 Tone & Style

Use for explanations, documentation, or anything reaching non-specialists. Also useful when an AI response has come back sounding like a legal brief when you wanted a friendly summary.

 
Write this in plain, clear English. Specific rules:
- No jargon or acronyms unless you explain them on first use
- Imagine you're explaining this to a smart friend with no background in the field
- Short sentences. Active voice. Direct.
- If there's a simpler word, use it. ("Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate.")
- No throat-clearing phrases ("It's worth noting that...", "It is important to understand...")

Clarity over impressiveness, every time.

🔗 Try combining with

🎨 Plain English + 🚧 Word Budget

Plain English strips the fluff, Word Budget enforces a hard limit. Together they're unstoppable for content that needs to be both clear and concise — landing pages, executive summaries, onboarding docs.

The Mentor Voice

▾
🎨 Tone & Style

Use for feedback requests, code reviews, draft critiques, or learning new skills. Changes the emotional tone of criticism from "here's what's wrong" to "here's how to get better."

 
Respond in the tone of a patient, experienced mentor who genuinely wants me to succeed. Specific guidelines:
- Be honest — don't soften real problems into meaninglessness
- Frame everything as "here's how to get better," not "here's what's wrong"
- Be encouraging without being false — I can handle the truth
- Point out strengths as well as gaps (both matter for improvement)
- When you spot something good, say so — specific praise lands better than generic

You're not cheerleading. You're coaching.

🔗 Try combining with

🎨 Mentor Voice + 📖 Draft → Critique → Revise

Add the Mentor Voice before using the Draft → Critique → Revise recipe from the Recipe Book. The recipe gives you the structure; the Mentor Voice ensures the critique actually helps you improve rather than making you want to quit.

🚧

Constraint & Guardrail Phrases

2 ingredients Keep the output on track and within bounds

The Fence

▾
🚧 Constraint

Use any time you've gotten an answer that drifted off-topic, over-caveated, or made wrong assumptions about your resources or situation. Append to any prompt to stake the boundaries.

 
As you respond, stay within these guardrails:
- Do NOT: [specific thing to avoid — e.g., give legal/medical/financial advice — explain the concept instead]
- Do NOT: assume I have [resource, time, or knowledge you don't have]
- Do NOT: pad the answer with excessive disclaimers or hedge every statement
- Do NOT: treat all options as equally valid when some are clearly better for my situation
- DO: stay focused on the specific ask — don't broaden the scope unless I ask you to

If you're genuinely uncertain about something, say so briefly and move on.

🔗 Try combining with

🚧 The Fence + 👤 Trusted Advisor

The Trusted Advisor sets up an expert persona; The Fence keeps that advisor from retreating into safe, generic guidance. Together they force a direct, contextually relevant response instead of the diplomatic non-answer you'd get otherwise.

The Word Budget

▾
🚧 Constraint

Use for anything going into a tight format — slide decks, email subject lines, social posts, press releases, or anywhere word count is a hard constraint rather than a preference.

 
Strict word budget for this response:
- Maximum total length: [X] words
- Prioritize cutting detail before cutting clarity
- Remove all filler phrases: "It's worth noting...", "In conclusion...", "Of course...", "It is important to..."
- Every sentence must earn its place — if it repeats or hedges unnecessarily, cut it
- Do not use bullet points just to pad — use them only if they genuinely help structure

If you genuinely cannot cover this topic in [X] words without losing critical meaning, say so in 1 sentence and ask what to prioritize — don't just silently go over budget.

🔗 Try combining with

🚧 Word Budget + 📐 Executive Brief + 🎨 Plain English

The ultimate tight-output stack. Executive Brief gives the structure, Plain English cuts jargon, Word Budget enforces the hard limit. Use this triple combo when you need to turn a wall of AI text into something a CFO would actually read.

💡

Pro tip: build a personal pantry

Copy your three most-used ingredients into a note or doc you keep open while working. After a few weeks, you'll have a personal starter kit — your own flavour of these blocks, tuned to your field and communication style.
