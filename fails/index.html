💥 Kitchen Disasters

The most instructive AI errors from our 27-AI shopping series. These aren't bugs — they're patterns. Understanding why they happen makes you a better prompter.

🎓

Why document AI failures?

Every failure here is a lesson. The same prompt was run through 27 AI tools, and the same errors kept appearing — often in the same ways. Recognising these patterns in your own AI responses means you can catch them before they matter. The errors aren't random; they're predictable, and predictable means preventable.

💻 The Laptop-as-Desktop Incident

Cohere Playground (Example 32) · Character.AI (Example 26) · The brief said "pre-built gaming PC"

The prompt asked for a pre-built gaming PC. Two AI tools recommended laptops instead.

❌ What was returned
Acer Nitro 5 (AN515-44) — $699.99 — 15.6" gaming laptop · Ryzen 5 5600H · GTX 1650 Ti 4GB · 8GB DDR4

Cohere Playground returned this as a Best Buy PC pick. It's a laptop with 8GB RAM and a GPU two generations below the RTX 3060 floor.

✅ What was asked for
A pre-built gaming PC… Best Buy first… RTX 3060 or better… 16GB RAM minimum

A desktop tower with an RTX 4060 and 16GB DDR4/DDR5, typically at Best Buy for $650–$750.

🎓

Why it happens — and the fix

Why: On Best Buy's website, gaming laptops and gaming desktops appear in the same search results for "gaming PC." Training data includes both. When an AI retrieves "gaming PC at Best Buy," it doesn't always apply the desktop constraint correctly.

Fix: Add "desktop tower" or "desktop form factor — not a laptop" explicitly to your prompt. "Pre-built gaming PC" alone isn't always sufficient. Also: always verify the model number — "AN515" is a dead giveaway for the Acer Nitro 5 laptop line.

The prompt said "at least 16GB of RAM" in plain English. Eleven or more AI responses returned at least one pick with 8GB RAM. This is the single most common spec error in the series.

"HP Pavilion Gaming Desktop — $699.99 — Ryzen 5 5600G, 8GB DDR4…"
— AI21 Studio Jamba Mini (Best Buy pick, Example 33)
"Acer Nitro 5 — $699.99 — 8GB DDR4…"
— Cohere Playground (Best Buy pick #2, Example 32)
"CyberPowerPC GXiXT8500B — $649.99 — 8GB DDR4…"
— Cohere Playground (Walmart pick, Example 32)
🎓

Why it keeps happening

Why: The HP Pavilion series and many budget gaming PCs are commonly sold with 8GB RAM as a base configuration. AI training data includes these listings as common "budget gaming PC" results. When an AI retrieves a system name, it may attach the base-tier specs rather than the upgraded version that actually exists in the price range.

Fix: Always scan the RAM spec on every single pick. 8GB in 2026 is a red flag at any gaming budget. If an AI returns an 8GB pick, ask it to remove any result under 16GB and regenerate. Or add "REJECT any system with less than 16GB RAM" in your prompt.

Why it happens

AI training data contains massive amounts of budget PC recommendations from 2019–2021 when the GTX 1650 and 1660 Super were the dominant budget options. Those systems are still listed on Amazon and Walmart's long-tail inventory. Models trained on older data, or models that retrieve stale product pages, surface these as "budget gaming PC" results without checking the GPU against current generation equivalents.

Fix: State your GPU floor explicitly in the prompt (as this series did), and still verify. If you see any GTX 16xx or 10xx GPU in a 2026 recommendation, treat it as a red flag. GPU model numbers are the most important single spec to check.

Silent VRAM risk

⚠️ The RTX 3050 6GB Trap

Venice.ai (4 picks) · MiniMax (2 picks) · Others — first identified by GLM in Example 23

The RTX 3050 comes in two variants: 8GB and 6GB. The 6GB variant is technically in the "RTX 30 series" family — so AIs sometimes include it as meeting an "RTX 3060 or better" spirit even though the brief excludes it. More importantly, Arc Raiders uses Unreal Engine 5, which has a practical VRAM minimum of 8GB for stable high-settings play.

"Acer Nitro 50 (~$720) — i5-14400F, RTX 3050 6GB, 16GB DDR5 — Top overall pick"
— Venice.ai (Example 30)
❌ RTX 3050 6GB
  • 6GB VRAM — below UE5 8GB+ recommendation
  • Narrower memory bus than the 8GB variant
  • Can cause texture pop-in and stuttering at high settings
  • Explicitly below the RTX 3060 floor the brief asked for
✅ RTX 3050 8GB (minimum acceptable)
  • 8GB VRAM — meets UE5 practical minimum
  • Wider memory bus than the 6GB variant
  • Playable at 1080p medium settings
  • Still below the RTX 3060 floor — but at least meets the VRAM threshold
🎓

The lesson: always check the VRAM variant, not just the GPU family

When evaluating a GPU pick, the model name alone (RTX 3050) isn't enough. The variant (6GB vs 8GB) matters significantly for VRAM-intensive titles. Always check the VRAM spec explicitly. For any UE5 game in 2026, treat 8GB VRAM as the practical minimum — not the headline GPU generation.

Tool mismatch

Six AI tools in the series failed the shopping brief — not because they're bad AIs, but because they're specialist tools used for the wrong job. Each one is excellent in its intended context.

Format ≠ accuracy

📋 Clean Table, Wrong Answer

AI21 Studio Jamba Mini (Example 33) · Cohere Playground (Example 32) · Beautiful formatting, wrong specs

Both AI21 and Cohere returned well-formatted responses with clean comparison tables, clear section headers, and structured per-pick breakdowns. Both also failed the GPU requirement on every single pick.

AI21 Jamba Mini response structure: ✅ Clean h3 headers per retailer · ✅ Formatted spec tables · ✅ "Why it fits" reasoning · ❌ RX 5500 XT (Best Buy) · ❌ GTX 1660 Super (Walmart) · ❌ GTX 1650 Super at $749 (Amazon)
🎓

The lesson: confident presentation is not a correctness signal

The most dangerous AI error isn't a vague or confused answer — it's a confidently wrong one presented in a polished format. A clean table makes specs easy to read. It doesn't make them accurate. Always verify the GPU model and RAM spec regardless of how well-structured the response looks. A tidy response that misses the spec floor is worse than a messy response that nails it, because the tidy one is more likely to fool you.

CPU age mismatch

🖥️ The CPU Time Warp

MiniMax M2.7 (Example 34) · Intel i7 8th Gen (2017) paired with RTX 3060 Ti (2020) at $749

MiniMax's Amazon pick listed an "Intel Core i7 8th Gen" CPU paired with an RTX 3060 Ti. The i7 8th Gen is a 2017–2018 processor — it's five CPU generations old in 2026. Pairing an old CPU with a mid-range GPU creates a performance ceiling that the GPU alone can't overcome.

❌ i7 8th Gen (2017) + RTX 3060 Ti
  • CPU bottleneck in CPU-intensive games
  • No PCIe 4.0 support (older platform)
  • Limited upgrade path
  • Listed vaguely — "i7 8th Gen" could be any of several chips
✅ Ryzen 5 7600 or i5-13400F (2022–2023)
  • Modern architecture, PCIe 4.0/5.0 support
  • No CPU bottleneck with RTX 4060-class GPUs
  • AM5 platform (7600) has upgrade path to Ryzen 9000
  • Series consensus CPU pairing for this budget
🎓

The lesson: always check both the CPU and GPU generations

GPU generation gets most of the attention in gaming PC research — but the CPU generation matters too. An "i7" label sounds impressive; "i7 8th Gen" is a 2017 chip that predates DDR5, PCIe 4.0, and modern IPC improvements by two full architectural generations. When evaluating any AI pick, check the CPU model number against current release dates. Any Intel CPU below 12th Gen or AMD CPU below Ryzen 5000 series deserves scrutiny in a 2026 purchase.
