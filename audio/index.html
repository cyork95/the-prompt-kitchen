🎵 Kitchen Radio

One prompt. Five AI music generators. All on the free tier. We ran the exact same request through Suno AI, Udio, Boomy, Meta MusicGen, and Stable Audio — and compared everything from lyrics to production quality.

🎯

The challenge prompt

Create an upbeat indie-pop song about a software developer who keeps getting distracted by side projects and never ships. The song should have a catchy chorus, self-deprecating humour, and a melody that feels like a Monday morning that turned into a Friday night. Include verses about coffee, half-finished GitHub repos, and the eternal optimism of "I'll finish it this weekend."

Same prompt, verbatim, across all five tools. All results generated on the free tier in May 2026.

Suno AI 🏆 Top Pick

suno.com · Free: 50 songs/day · Full vocal songs with lyrics

Try Suno →

Suno is the closest thing to a complete song factory. Give it a prompt and it returns a fully produced track — vocals, instruments, lyrics, and mix — in about 30 seconds. The free tier gives you 50 songs per day, which is genuinely generous. Songs are capped at around 4 minutes and you get two variations per generation.

What it did well

  • Understood the indie-pop brief completely
  • Wrote genuine lyrics about coffee, GitHub repos, and weekends
  • The chorus is actually catchy — earworm territory
  • Production quality rivals amateur-label releases
  • Two variations gave a choice of energy level

Limitations

  • Can't edit individual sections — regenerate the whole track
  • Lyrics occasionally go off-brief in verse 2
  • Commercial use requires paid plan (~$8/mo)
Prompt tip: Add style tags in brackets to steer the sound. [indie pop, jangly guitars, lo-fi drums, upbeat] gives Suno a much clearer target than a plain description.
🔵

Udio

udio.com · Free: 10 songs/day · Lyrics + instrumental, section-by-section editing

Try Udio →

Udio is Suno's closest rival — and in some ways its superior when it comes to stylistic range. Where Suno leans polished and radio-ready, Udio can veer into weirder, more experimental territory. It also offers section-by-section generation (intro, verse, chorus, outro separately) which gives you much more editorial control at the cost of more clicks.

What it did well

  • More stylistic range than Suno — can go left-field
  • Section editing lets you rebuild specific parts
  • Lyrics are creative and often surprising
  • Handles unusual genre mashups well

Limitations

  • Only 10 free songs/day — much tighter than Suno
  • Prompt adherence drifts more on specific lyric themes
  • Section-by-section workflow takes longer
  • Occasional audio glitches at section joins
Prompt tip: Udio responds well to explicit section labels. Try writing: "[Verse] Monday morning, third coffee down… [Chorus] I'll ship it this weekend, I swear…" and it will follow your structure closely.
🟣

Boomy

boomy.com · Free: 25 songs/month · Genre-based generation, monetisation-first

Try Boomy →

Boomy takes a very different approach from Suno and Udio. Rather than text-prompt generation, it works from genre selection — you pick a style (Pop, EDM, Hip Hop, etc.), click generate, and it creates a short instrumental track. The interesting twist is that Boomy is explicitly designed for music monetisation: you can submit your tracks to streaming platforms. But as a prompt-driven creative tool, it's the most limited of the five.

  • Fastest time to a usable track (under 10 seconds)
  • Consistent, clean production quality
  • Great for background/stock music use cases
  • Built-in streaming platform distribution

Limitations

  • No text prompt — genre selection only
  • No lyrics generation
  • 25 songs/month free is very restrictive
  • Tracks feel formulaic — limited creative range
  • Can't test our exact brief — wrong tool for lyric-driven songs
🤗

Meta MusicGen

huggingface.co/spaces/facebook/MusicGen · Free, unlimited · Open source, instrumental only

Try MusicGen →

Meta's MusicGen is a fully open-source music generation model that runs through Hugging Face Spaces. It's the most technically transparent of the five — you can see the model weights, run it locally, and use it commercially. The catch: it generates instrumental music only (no lyrics, no vocals). It's research-grade rather than consumer-grade, and the UI is bare-bones compared to Suno or Udio.

What it did well

  • Completely free, no account required
  • Open source — can run locally or commercially

  • Good for developers and researchers wanting full control

Limitations

  • Instrumental only — no vocals or lyrics
  • Generation capped at 30 seconds on the HF Space
  • Queue times on HF Space can be slow (1–5 minutes)
  • No polished consumer UI
  • Can't test lyric-driven brief directly
Who it's for: MusicGen is ideal if you need royalty-free instrumental beds, want to run music AI locally, or are building something on top of a music generation model. Not the right tool for song creation.
🌊

Stable Audio

stableaudio.com · Free: 20 songs/day · Stability AI · Instrumental + stems

Try Stable Audio →

Stable Audio comes from Stability AI (the team behind Stable Diffusion) and brings a similar philosophy: high-quality generation with serious attention to audio fidelity. Like MusicGen, it's instrumental-only — no vocals. But where MusicGen feels research-grade, Stable Audio feels like a proper product, with a clean interface, controllable duration (up to 3 minutes), and notably high audio quality in the final output.

  • Controllable duration (up to 3 minutes)
  • Responds to detailed instrument and production descriptors
  • 20 free generations/day is workable
  • Good for sound design, score beds, and stems

Limitations

  • Instrumental only — no vocals
  • Can't follow a lyric-driven prompt
  • Less creative range than Suno or Udio on genre-bending requests

The verdict

If you want a complete song with vocals and lyrics that matches a specific brief, Suno AI is the clear winner — especially on the free tier where you get 50 generations a day. Udio is a close second with more stylistic range but tighter daily limits.

If you need instrumental music for video, podcasts, or background use, Stable Audio produces the cleanest results and MusicGen is the choice if you need something open-source and royalty-clear. Boomy fills a different niche entirely — it's a music monetisation tool that happens to generate songs, not a creative prompt tool.

💡

For this challenge specifically

Only Suno and Udio could actually follow the lyric-driven brief about a developer with side-project ADHD. The other three are inherently instrumental tools — you'd need to combine them with a separate lyrics generation step (e.g., write lyrics in Claude, generate backing track in Stable Audio, then record or use a voice AI for vocals). That's a more complex workflow but gives you more control over each element.

🎙️ Try it yourself

Run the same prompt through any of these tools and compare your results. The prompt is designed to be specific enough that differences between tools are immediately obvious — pay attention to:

  • Whether the lyrics actually mention coffee, GitHub, and weekends (prompt adherence)
  • How catchy the chorus is (musical quality)
  • Whether the mood matches "Monday morning that turned into Friday night" (style interpretation)
