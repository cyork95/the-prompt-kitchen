Beginner Everyday
Model: Claude Sonnet · Task: Email rewrite

Turning a defensive email into a professional one

▾

A project manager needed to respond to a client complaint without sounding defensive or dismissive — a tonal balancing act that AI handles well.

Prompt used
Here's my rough draft of a reply to a client who complained that our last deliverable had errors:

"Hi Sarah, I've reviewed the document and I don't think the errors were as significant as you're suggesting. Some of them were actually caused by changes you made to the spec after we started. We did flag this at the time. I've attached the corrected version. Let me know if there's anything else."

Please improve this email. I want to:
- Acknowledge her frustration genuinely (not with empty phrases)
- Mention the spec changes factually, without it sounding like I'm blaming her
- Come across as professional, solution-focused, and relationship-preserving
- Keep it concise — under 120 words
Tell me what you changed and why.
AI Output (Claude Sonnet)

Revised email:

Hi Sarah,

Thank you for flagging this — I've reviewed the document and you're right that the errors shouldn't have been there. I've attached a corrected version.

For context, a few of the changes trace back to the spec update in week three, which shifted some of the original requirements. We should have been clearer about how that would affect the output — that's on us to manage better.

Happy to jump on a quick call if it would help. I want to make sure you're confident in what we're delivering.

— Cody York

What changed: Opened with acknowledgment rather than defense. Mentioned the spec change as context (not blame) and immediately owned the communication failure around it. Ended with a relationship-forward offer rather than a perfunctory sign-off.

💡

Why this worked

The prompt gave specific tonal goals ("solution-focused, relationship-preserving") rather than just asking to "improve it." Asking AI to explain its changes after the fact is also a good habit — it helps you learn the reasoning, not just copy the output.

Beginner Everyday
Model: ChatGPT GPT-4o · Task: Concept explanation

Understanding a confusing medical concept

▾

A patient was confused by their doctor's explanation of "insulin resistance." They used AI to build a genuine understanding before their follow-up appointment.

Prompt used
My doctor told me I have insulin resistance and should watch my diet. I nodded but I honestly don't understand what it means or why diet matters.

Explain insulin resistance to me like I'm a retired teacher with no medical background. Use a concrete analogy — ideally something from everyday life. Tell me:
1. What's actually happening in my body
2. Why diet specifically matters (not just "eat healthy" — why does it connect to this condition)
3. What I should actually ask my doctor at my next appointment

No jargon without explanation. I want to understand this, not just be told what to do.
AI Output (GPT-4o — condensed)

The analogy: Think of insulin like a key, and your cells like houses with locked doors. Normally, insulin (the key) fits perfectly into the lock and lets glucose (energy) inside. With insulin resistance, it's as if the locks have gotten stiff — the keys still work, but your pancreas has to make many more keys than it used to just to get the same amount of glucose inside.

Why diet matters: Every time you eat carbohydrates or sugar, your blood glucose rises and your body needs to send out insulin keys. If you eat in ways that cause big, frequent spikes — lots of refined carbs, sugary drinks, large portions — your body is constantly flooding the system with insulin. Over time, this makes the "stiff lock" problem worse. Eating in ways that cause gentler, steadier glucose rises gives your system a chance to recover...

Questions to ask your doctor: 1) What specific dietary changes matter most for my situation? 2) Is my level of insulin resistance likely to be reversible with lifestyle changes? 3) Should I see a dietitian, or is this something we can manage with general guidance?

💡

Why this worked

Specifying "retired teacher with no medical background" gave the model a precise level to pitch at. Asking for an analogy from everyday life — and stating that preference explicitly — produced something genuinely intuitive rather than a textbook definition. Ending with "questions to ask my doctor" turns the explanation into action.

Model: Claude Sonnet · Task: Research → Plan → Implement

Planning a career pivot from finance to UX design

▾

Someone with 8 years in financial analysis wanted to move into UX design and had no idea where to start. They used the Research → Plan → Implement pattern.

Prompt used
I want to transition from financial analysis to UX design. I'm not sure of the best approach yet.

First, help me understand the landscape: What are the realistic paths into UX from a non-design background? What do hiring managers actually look for? What are the mistakes career-switchers typically make?

Then, based on that, suggest a concrete plan for my situation: I have 8 years in financial analysis, strong analytical skills, no design training, a full-time job I can't leave yet, and roughly 10 hours per week to dedicate to this transition. I'm 34 and based in the UK.

Finally, give me the first 3 actionable steps I can take this month.
AI Output (Claude Sonnet — condensed)

The landscape: The most common entry paths are: bootcamps (3–6 months, intensive, expensive), self-study with a strong portfolio (6–18 months, cheaper, harder to stay accountable), and hybrid approaches using structured online courses while building portfolio work. Hiring managers care far more about your portfolio than your credentials — specifically, they want to see 3–5 case studies that show your process: how you identified a problem, how you researched users, how you iterated on solutions. The common mistake career-switchers make is getting certified but not building real work.

Your plan: Your analytical background is a genuine advantage — UX research and data-driven design thinking are highly valued. I'd suggest: Month 1-2: Build foundational knowledge (Google UX Design Certificate on Coursera — $50/month, ~10 hrs/week fits your schedule). Month 3-6: Start a portfolio project redesigning something you use at work. Month 7-12: Freelance one small project for free to get real client experience and a case study...

Your first 3 steps this month: 1) Enroll in the Google UX Certificate and complete module one. 2) Create a simple portfolio site (Notion or Cargo work fine at this stage). 3) Identify one specific digital product you use regularly that has a clear usability problem — this becomes your first portfolio project.

💡

Why this worked

The three-stage structure forced the AI to give context before jumping to solutions — which produced a plan grounded in reality rather than generic advice. Including very specific constraints (10 hrs/week, full-time job, UK-based, 34 years old) made the plan feel genuinely personal rather than copied from a career blog.

Technical Intermediate
Model: Claude Sonnet · Task: Code debugging

Fixing a Python data processing script that silently produces wrong results

▾

A data analyst's script ran without errors but produced incorrect totals. Silent bugs like this are among the hardest to debug alone — AI spotted it immediately.

Prompt used
My Python script runs without errors but the sales totals it produces are wrong — they're about 15% too high and I can't figure out why.

Language: Python 3.11, using pandas
What it should do: aggregate daily sales by region, excluding refunded orders
What it's doing: producing totals that are consistently higher than expected

Here's the relevant code:

df_sales = pd.read_csv('sales.csv')
df_filtered = df_sales[df_sales['status'] != 'refunded']
totals = df_filtered.groupby('region')['amount'].sum()
print(totals)

Don't just fix it — explain what's wrong and why, so I understand it for next time. Also tell me how I could have caught this earlier.
AI Output (Claude Sonnet)

The problem: Your filter df_sales['status'] != 'refunded' is case-sensitive. If your CSV contains values like 'Refunded', 'REFUNDED', or 'Refunded ' (with a trailing space), those rows won't be caught by the filter and their amounts will still be included in your totals. That would explain a consistent ~15% inflation if roughly that proportion of orders have mismatched status strings.

The fix: df_filtered = df_sales[df_sales['status'].str.strip().str.lower() != 'refunded'] — this strips whitespace and lowercases before comparing.

How you could have caught this earlier: A quick df_sales['status'].value_counts() before filtering would have shown you all the unique values in the status column and immediately revealed any casing or spacing variations. Making this a habit whenever you filter on a string column will save you hours.

💡

Why this worked

Asking AI to explain the root cause and how to prevent it next time turned a one-off fix into a lasting lesson. The "don't just fix it" instruction is essential — without it, many AI models just hand you the corrected code with no explanation.

Creative Intermediate
Model: Claude Sonnet · Task: Creative writing with style matching

Writing a short story opening in a specific voice

▾

A writer used few-shot examples to get AI to match their established story voice instead of producing generic fiction.

Prompt used
I'm writing a collection of short stories in a specific voice. Here are two openings from stories I've already written so you can match the style:

Story 1 opening:
"The day my father taught me to drive, he cried twice. Once when I stalled at the roundabout on Elm Street, and once when I didn't."

Story 2 opening:
"She kept the note in her shoe for three years. Not because she reread it — she hadn't — but because she needed to know exactly where it was."

Now write an opening paragraph for a new story with this premise: A woman discovers that her recently deceased mother had a second phone she knew nothing about.

Match the voice exactly — the restraint, the specificity, the emotional weight carried by small details. Don't over-explain. No melodrama.
AI Output (Claude Sonnet)

"The phone was in the left pocket of the winter coat, the one her mother hadn't worn since February. It had 34% battery. That was the part that stayed with her — not the texts she would eventually read, not the name she didn't recognise, but the 34%. Someone had charged it not so long ago."

💡

Why this worked

Providing two concrete style examples (few-shot prompting) is dramatically more effective than describing a style in abstract terms. "Restraint, specificity, emotional weight in small details" as instructions alone would produce something generic. The examples gave the model something precise to calibrate against.

Advanced Technical
Model: Claude Opus · Task: Strategic analysis with steelmanning

Stress-testing a business decision before committing

▾

A founder used the Devil's Advocate pattern before making a significant hiring decision, and discovered a risk she hadn't considered.

Prompt used
I'm about to hire a Head of Sales for my B2B SaaS startup (12 people, $800K ARR, Series A in 6 months). I've found a candidate I'm excited about — 10 years experience, big-company background (Salesforce, then a $50M ARR startup), strong references. I'm planning to offer the role.

Play devil's advocate as hard as you can. What are the strongest arguments against this hire? What risks am I probably not weighting correctly? What questions should I have asked but haven't? Don't soften it — I need to stress-test this before committing.
AI Output (Claude Opus — condensed)

The culture-fit risk you're probably underweighting: A candidate whose entire career has been at $50M+ companies has never had to build a sales motion from scratch. At Salesforce, the brand does half the selling. At a $50M ARR startup, there's a playbook and a team. At $800K ARR, there's neither. The skill of hunting enterprise deals with no brand, no case studies, and no SDR team is genuinely different — and many people who excel in the former struggle deeply with the latter. Did you test for this specifically, or did the impressive pedigree carry the interviews?

The Series A timing is a landmine: If this person joins 3 months before your raise, they'll spend that time learning your business, not driving pipeline. You'll present to investors with a shiny new sales hire who hasn't produced anything yet — which can actually be a yellow flag if not handled carefully. Alternatively, a bad first 90 days could tank your confidence right before the raise.

Questions you may not have asked: Walk me through a deal you won at a company with no brand recognition and no existing customer base. What was your average deal size and sales cycle at [the startup] vs. what I'd realistically expect here? Have you ever had to fire someone you hired? What happened?

💡

Why this worked

The founder told me afterward that the Series A timing point was something she genuinely hadn't thought through. The "don't soften it" instruction is critical — without it, AI tends to hedge and present criticism as "things to consider" rather than genuine challenges. Asking for risks you're "not weighting correctly" is more useful than asking for a list of risks, because it focuses on the blind spots, not the obvious ones.

Planning a meaningful birthday gift for a hard-to-shop-for parent

I need help finding the perfect birthday gift for my 78-year-old father. He is impossible to shop for.

My budget: £75-£150. I want something he'll actually use and enjoy, not something that'll sit in a cupboard.

Please give me 5 specific, creative suggestions — not generic "gift experience" ideas, but things a thoughtful daughter who knows him well would suggest. For each one, tell me where to actually buy it in the UK.

Getting the specific recordings he grew up with, pressed as a proper LP, sits perfectly at the intersection of his love of music and his engineering appreciation for physical media.

💡

Why this worked

Asking for UK-specific purchasing options made the results immediately actionable rather than requiring another round of Googling.

Intermediate Everyday
Model: Claude Sonnet 4.6 (Extended Thinking) · Task: Research + shopping recommendation

Finding the best budget gaming PC for a specific game across multiple retailers

▾

A buyer needed a pre-built gaming PC under $800 that could run a specific upcoming game. The prompt gave precise constraints — budget, game, retailers in priority order, and exact output format — and got back real product links, a comparison table, and a concrete pick.

Prompt used
I'm looking for a budget pre-built PC with the following requirements:

Budget: $400–$800

Primary Use Cases:
- Light gaming (main game: Arc Raiders)
- General computing tasks

Hardware Priorities:
- Specs capable of running Arc Raiders smoothly (look up recommended system requirements)
- Good price-to-performance ratio within budget

Nice to Have:
- Keyboard and mouse bundle included

Preferred Retailers (in order of preference):
- Best Buy
- Walmart
- Amazon

Please include for each recommendation: the retailer, price, key specs (CPU, GPU, RAM, storage), and why it fits Arc Raiders specifically.
AI Output (Claude Sonnet 4.6 with Extended Thinking — condensed)

The model first confirmed Arc Raiders' recommended specs from official sources (RTX 2070 / RX 5700 XT, i5-9600K / Ryzen 5 3600, 16GB RAM) before evaluating any PCs — then surfaced three options, one per retailer:

Best Buy — iBUYPOWER Scale (~$799, watch for sales to $699): RTX 4060, i5-14400F, 16GB DDR5, 1TB NVMe. RGB keyboard & mouse included. "RTX 4060 brings DLSS 3 support — Arc Raiders supports DLSS, with DLSS 4 Multi Frame Generation boosting FPS significantly for RTX users."

Walmart — Skytech Shadow (~$799): RTX 4060, i5-12400F (12th gen), 16GB DDR5, 1TB NVMe. Gaming K+M included, 1-year warranty. Best value if the iBUYPOWER is over budget at time of purchase.

Amazon — CyberPowerPC Gamer Xtreme (~$799–$849): RTX 4060, i5-14400F, 16GB DDR5, 2TB NVMe (double the others). K+M included, lifetime tech support. Best pick if storage is a priority.

Final recommendation: "If the iBUYPOWER Scale is $799 or less at Best Buy at time of purchase, grab it — Best Buy's return policy and in-store support is hard to beat. If it's sitting at full $999, the Walmart Skytech Shadow at $799 is essentially the same gaming experience for less."

💡

Why this worked — and how to take it further

Three things made this prompt unusually effective: structured constraints (budget range, ranked retailers, specific game) gave the model a clear brief rather than an open-ended question. Asking it to look up requirements first meant the recommendations were grounded in real specs rather than guesses. And requesting a specific output format (retailer, price, key specs, game-specific reasoning) ensured the answer was scannable rather than a wall of text.

For high-stakes purchases, consider running the same prompt across multiple AI tools — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini — and comparing results. Where they agree, you can be confident. Where they disagree, dig into why. You can even paste all three responses back into one AI and ask it to synthesise a final consensus recommendation.

Walmart — CyberPowerPC Gamer Xtreme (~$799): i5-14400F, RTX 4060 8GB, 16GB DDR5, 1TB SSD. K+M included. "RTX 4060 crushes the RTX 2070 recommended spec... should do 1080p Ultra 60fps+ easily."

Amazon — Skytech Archangel (~$749–$799): Ryzen 5 5600, GTX 1660 Super 6GB, 16GB DDR4, 1TB NVMe. K+M included. "GTX 1660 Super sits between minimum GTX 1050 Ti and recommended RTX 2070... handles 1080p Medium/High."

💡

What differed — and what that tells you

Different products surfaced. Claude recommended RTX 4060 machines across all three retailers. Meta AI recommended a more varied lineup — RTX 4060 at Walmart, but RX 6600 at Best Buy and GTX 1660 Super at Amazon. Neither is wrong; they reflect different weighting of GPU tier vs. price.

Meta AI added warnings Claude didn't. The "What to Avoid" section and budget-tier reality check are genuinely useful context — the kind of thing an experienced buyer would tell you. Claude focused tighter on the brief; Meta AI gave more unsolicited context.

Tone differed noticeably. Meta AI opened with "without crying" and matched a casual register. Claude stayed professional and table-formatted. Same prompt, different personality — worth knowing when you choose a tool for a task.

The synthesis move: with two AI responses in hand, the next step is to paste both into a third AI and ask: "Here are two AI recommendations for the same brief. Where do they agree? Where do they disagree, and which recommendation has stronger reasoning? Give me a final consolidated pick." That's where the real value emerges.
